Short answer: no, not yet. Three trials is way too thin to act on, even with a delta that looks this clean. With n=3 per arm, a 40→80% shift could easily be noise from model stochasticity, prompt-order effects, or a lucky seed. You don't have enough signal to distinguish "this skill works" from "I happened to catch a good run." Before shipping, you need more trials and some scrutiny of what actually happened in the transcripts.

Concretely, bump your sample size substantially — aim for at least n=10 per arm, and ideally 20+ if the eval is cheap to run. At n=10 vs n=10, a 40%→80% delta starts becoming statistically defensible; at n=3 vs n=3 it's essentially anecdote. While you're at it, vary the seed/temperature if your harness supports it, and make sure baseline and treatment trials are interleaved rather than run in two back-to-back batches (otherwise you risk confounds from model drift, caching, or time-of-day effects on the provider side). Record the raw pass/fail per trial, not just the aggregate rate, so you can inspect variance.

Then — and this is the part people skip — actually read the transcripts of the treatment passes and the baseline failures. The Iron Law framing in arcforge's skill PR checklist (`.claude/rules/git-workflow.md`) exists precisely for this: you need to articulate (1) what baseline behavior you observed (RED), (2) how the skill addresses those specific failures (GREEN), and (3) what loopholes you closed (REFACTOR). If you can't point to a concrete failure mode in the baseline transcripts that the skill's instructions directly address, an 80% pass rate might mean the skill is accidentally leaking the answer, over-constraining the task, or gaming the eval's scoring criteria rather than actually teaching the behavior you care about. Per the feedback in auto-memory (`feedback_eval_driven_skill_dev.md`), data-driven proof is required — but that cuts both ways: the data has to be robust and the mechanism has to be understood, not just the aggregate number looking good.

Next steps, in order: (1) re-run with n>=10 per arm, interleaved; (2) inspect baseline failure transcripts and confirm your skill targets those exact failure modes; (3) check that treatment passes actually exhibit the behavior you designed for (not just a different path to pass); (4) if all three hold, then draft the PR with the RED/GREEN/REFACTOR writeup and ship.
